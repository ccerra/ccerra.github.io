Crown shyness

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

For the album by Trash Boat, see Crown Shyness (album).

Crown shyness(also canopy disengagement,[1] canopy shyness,[2] or inter-crown spacing[3]) is a phenomenon observed in some tree species, in which the crowns of fully stocked trees do not touch each other, forming a canopy with channel-like gaps. [4][5] The phenomenon is most prevalent among trees of the same species, but also occurs between trees of different species. [6][7] There exist many hypotheses as to why crown shyness is an adaptive behavior, and research suggests that it might inhibit spread of leaf-eating insect larvae. [8]

Possible physiological explanations

The exact physiological cause of tree shyness is uncertain. [ 6 ] The phenomenon has been discussed in the scientific literature since the 1920s. [ 9 ] The variety of hypotheses and experimental results could suggest that there are multiple mechanisms in different species, an example of convergent evolution . Some hypotheses state that the interlacing of canopy branches leads to " reciprocal pruning " between adjacent trees. Trees located in windy areas suffer physical damage when they collide with each other during winds . As a result of collisions and abrasions, a shy crown shape is induced. Certain studies suggest that the growth of lateral branches is little influenced by neighboring trees until a disturbance from mechanical abrasion occurs. [ 10 ] If crowns are artificially prevented from colliding during winds, they gradually fill the gaps in the canopy. [ 11 ] This interpretation explains instances of shyness between branches of the same tree. Proponents of this idea mention that shyness is particularly observed in conditions conducive to this type of pruning, such as windy forests, forests with flexible trees, and forests in primary succession where branches are flexible and have restricted lateral movement. [ 6 ] [ 12 ] Based on this interpretation, variable flexibility in lateral branches is closely related to the degree of shyness.
